Before you can write a good argument, you need to see it. A Logic Map is a simple diagram that lets you lay out your position, your reasons, your examples, and the counter-argument — all on one page, before you write a single sentence. It extends the O-R-E-S speaking template you already know into something sharper: a map that also accounts for what the other side might say.
Why use a Logic Map?
It reveals weak spots early. If you cannot fill in "Example" under your Reason, your argument is probably too abstract. Fix it on the map — not halfway through writing.
It forces you to meet the counter-argument. A strong academic answer does not ignore the other side. It names it, then responds to it.
The four parts of a Logic Map
Thesis
Your main position. One clear sentence.
Reason
The strongest reason supporting your thesis.
Example
A real, specific situation — not a general claim.
Counter + Response
What critics would say, and why you still hold your view.
Worked example
Sample prompt
"Should schools ban smartphones in classrooms?"
Completed Logic Map
THESIS: Schools should ban smartphones during class but allow them during breaks. ├─ Reason: Phones fragment attention even when │ students are not actively using them. │ └─ Example: In my class, a teacher ran a one-week │ phone-free trial. Discussion got noticeably │ more focused — even quieter students spoke up. │ └─ Counter: Phones can be useful learning tools (dictionaries, research, translation). └─ My response: Those uses are real but rare in a typical class. A shared classroom tablet or laptop can handle them without the constant notification pull a phone has.

What makes a Logic Map work

  • The Thesis is one sentence — and clear enough that someone could disagree with it.
  • The Example is concrete (a specific moment, class, or experience) — not a generalisation.
  • The Counter-argument is the strongest version of the opposing view, not a straw man.
  • The Response does not dismiss the counter — it acknowledges what is true in it, then explains why your position still holds.

Before you finish — self-check

  • Can someone reasonably disagree with your Thesis? If not, it is too vague — sharpen it.
  • Is your Example a specific moment from your life, school, or community? Avoid "in general" or "many people say".
  • Did you write the Counter-argument in a way the other side would actually agree with? If they would say "that is not what we mean," rewrite it.
  • Does your Response show why your view still holds after hearing the counter — not just repeat your Thesis?
A note on doing this well: A filled map with short, specific lines is much more useful than a map with long, vague ones. If you find yourself writing long sentences to fill each blank, stop and ask: "What is the core of what I am trying to say?" — then write just that.
