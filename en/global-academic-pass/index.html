About This Course

What Is Global Academic Pass?

Global Academic Pass (GAP) takes everything you love about TOMODACHI English — learning alongside peers from around the world — and pushes the conversation further. From everyday topics like food and school life, we move into social and academic themes: environment, technology, education. You debate real issues with international classmates, sharpening your logical and academic English along the way.

The ORES framework teaches you to structure your opinions — opinion, reason, example, summary — so you can respond to any question with clarity and confidence. Sessions also follow the format of IELTS*, the world's leading English proficiency exam, covering both speaking and writing. For students with study abroad or overseas university goals, GAP gives you real, structured practice toward those ambitions.

Talking through future plans and university options with peers from other countries has a way of making those paths feel real. That is what GAP is designed to do.

*IELTS: The International English Language Testing System — the world's most widely used English proficiency test for study abroad, work, and migration.

  Logic Template: O-R-E-S

IELTS Speaking Part 3 demands the ability to develop an opinion. These 4 steps let you answer any question with structure and confidence.

O

Opinion

I believe that... / In my view...

R

Reason

This is because... / The main reason is...

E

Example

For instance... / In my country, for example...

S

Summary

Therefore... / That is why I think...

  Model Answer Using O-R-E-S

Question: "Do you think online learning is better than classroom learning?"

O In my view, online learning has real advantages for motivated students.
R The main reason is that it allows learners to study at their own pace and revisit difficult content.
E For instance, when I struggled with a grammar point, I was able to rewatch the explanation three times — something impossible in a regular classroom.
S That is why I believe online learning can be highly effective, provided the student is self-disciplined.
  Academic Vocabulary: Upgrade Your Words

Swapping everyday words for more precise, academic alternatives can dramatically change how you come across as a speaker.

Instead of "good"
Beneficial
"It is beneficial because..."
Instead of "bad"
Harmful
"This could be harmful to..."
Instead of "good"
Essential
"It is essential for students to..."
Instead of "bad"
Challenging
"One challenging aspect is..."
Instead of "I think":   "I am of the opinion that..."  /  "I would argue that..."

  Beyond ORES — Key Concepts You Build in GAP

ORES is the foundation of every session, but each class adds a new thinking and expression framework — called a Key Concept. Here are some examples of what you develop in GAP.

  Speaking

  • Making Examples Specific
    Narrow "a friend" to "my friend Rina, who chose a local university" — concrete details make your argument far more persuasive.
  • Hedging Language
    Replace "it is..." with "it tends to..." or "in some cases..." for a more measured, academically credible tone.
  • Responding to Counter-Arguments
    Address "But what about...?" calmly and logically, while holding your position.
  • Paraphrasing the Question
    Rephrase the question in your own words before answering — shows comprehension and buys thinking time.
  • Signposting
    Use discourse markers ("First," "Next," "Finally") so listeners can follow your logic at every step.

  Writing

  • Spoken → Written Conversion
    Turn your spoken ORES into a paragraph by adding connectors (Firstly, For instance…).
  • Topic Sentences
    Open each paragraph with a single sentence that states exactly what it is about.
  • Concession Structure
    "While X is understandable, I believe Y" — acknowledge the opposing view, then assert your own.
  • Deepening the Logic Map
    Add a second reason (Body 2) to build a stronger, more complete argument.
  • Peer Review
    Move beyond "good job" — give specific praise and one concrete suggestion for improvement.
  • Full Essay Structure
    Learn how an introduction + Body 1 + Body 2 + conclusion connect as a unified piece.
