2. O-R-E-S Quick Recap (3 mins)

A fast reminder — and a welcome for anyone joining the course this week.

Opinion

"In my view…" — What you believe.

Reason

"The main reason is…" — Why you believe it.

Example

"For instance…" — A real situation that supports it.

Summary

"That is why…" — One sentence to close.

Full reference: see the O-R-E-S & Logic Map sheet.

4. Key Concept: Spoken O-R-E-S → Written Paragraph (10 mins)

Last week, you used O-R-E-S when you spoke. Today, the same four steps become a written paragraph. The structure stays — but four things change:

  1. You need an explicit Topic Sentence. In speech, your Opinion can arrive softly. In writing, the first sentence must announce it clearly.
  2. Linking words become visible. Firstly, For instance, Consequently. In speech these are optional. In writing, the reader needs them.
  3. The reader cannot ask "Why?" So you must anticipate the question and answer it inside your paragraph.
  4. The tone becomes more formal. "I would argue that" instead of "I think." "Consequently" instead of "So."
Today's target: Introduction (2–3 sentences) + Body 1 (4–5 sentences) = about 100 words
~3 mins Delivery
"Same structure, different mode. When you speak, your voice, your pauses, your expression all help the listener follow. When you write, none of that is there — just words on a page. So we need four small adjustments."
Read each numbered point with one quick example. Do not over-explain. The point will land when they see the demo on the next slide.
Reassurance
"Look at the target — only about 100 words. This is not a full essay. This is the opening move, done well."
⚠ Do not introduce Body 2 or Conclusion. If a student asks, say: "That comes after you master this first move."

4. Demo — From Logic Map to Strong Paragraph

Step 1: Before writing, this person built a Logic Map:

THESIS: University should be free for students who meet academic requirements ├─ Reason 1: Creates equal opportunity │ └─ Example: My classmate chose a cheaper school — could not afford fees └─ Counter: Tax burden on society └─ Response: Long-term economic benefit outweighs the cost

Step 2: Then they turned it into a paragraph:

Strong — Model

The question of whether university should be free is widely debated. OIn my view, university should be free for all students who meet the academic requirements, because education should depend on ability, not on family wealth. RTo begin with, free university creates equal opportunity. EFor instance, a classmate of mine chose a local college over her first-choice university simply because her family could not afford the fees. SConsequently, society loses the potential of capable young people, and this is why I would argue that removing financial barriers is essential.

  THESIS → Reason → Example in the Map became O → R → E → S in the paragraph. The difference between Weak and Strong is not talent — it is planning.
Note: This model paragraph covers Introduction + Body 1 only. The Counter-argument from the Logic Map is saved for Body 2 — we are not writing that today.
~4 mins The "aha" moment
Show the Logic Map first. Point at each branch: "THESIS at the top — that becomes the Opinion. Reason 1 — that becomes the Reason. Example — that becomes the Example. And the Counter-argument — this person thought about the other side before writing."
Then show the Strong paragraph. Read it aloud and point at the O-R-E-S tags: "Same person, same topic as the Weak paragraph. But they made a Map first, and now a reader can follow every step."
Key message
"The difference between the Weak and Strong paragraph is not talent. It is planning. The Logic Map is the planning tool. In Practice 1, you are going to build one of these with a partner."
Assistant Real Talk (~2 mins)
"Assistant — when you write essays or reports, whether for IELTS, university, or work, what was harder than you expected: coming up with the ideas, or organizing them clearly on the page?"
Most people say organization was the hard part. This applies beyond IELTS — university essays, research papers, and professional writing all require the same logical structure. Reinforce the message: thinking clearly before writing is the real academic skill.
⚠ Do not break down every sentence of the model. Point at the tags and the map — that is enough.

5. Cohesive Devices Toolkit

These linking words are essential for academic writing — they guide your reader through your argument. They are also one of the four IELTS Task 2 scoring criteria (Coherence & Cohesion), so mastering them improves both your academic skills and your test performance.

To open Body 1 — the Reason slot

"Firstly, …"  /  "To begin with, …"  /  "The main reason is that…"

To introduce an Example

"For instance, …"  /  "A clear example of this is…"  /  "In my experience, …"

To link cause and effect — the Summary slot

"Consequently, …"  /  "As a result, …"  /  "This is why I would argue that…"

For your Opinion — carried over from Lesson 1

"In my view, …"  /  "I would argue that…"

  This toolkit is shared in the Zoom chat — keep it open while you write.
~2 mins Delivery
"These are not fancy words. They are signals. 'Firstly' tells the reader: here comes my reason. 'For instance': here comes my proof. 'Consequently': here is the result. Four signals, and your reader never gets lost."
Read 2–3 examples aloud. Do not drill. Students will pick up the rest when drafting.
Share the toolkit in chat NOW so it is already visible when breakouts open.

6. Practice 1 — Logic Co-Construction (15 mins)

"Should university education be free for everyone? Give your opinion."
Same prompt as last week's homework. Students with a draft may use it as a starting point.

Mode: Challenger Pair — this is NOT brainstorming

  • Presenter (A) — Share your opinion using your Logic Map. If you did not do the homework, build your map as you speak.
  • Challenger (B) — Your job is not to agree. Ask "Why?", "Can you give a real example?", "What about the other side?" Push until your partner's argument can survive.

Steps

  • Round 1 (6 min) — A presents, B challenges. Refine the Logic Map together in the shared doc.
  • Round 2 (6 min) — Switch roles.
  • Return to main room (2 min) — Instructor shows 1–2 Logic Maps.
See next slide for Challenger Questions, Logic Map, and Dialogue example — show this before releasing to breakout rooms.
~1 min Setup — the most important instruction of today
"Here is the key rule for the next 15 minutes. You are not brainstorming. You are not agreeing. One of you is Presenter — share your Logic Map from the homework. The other is Challenger — your job is to make your partner's argument stronger by questioning it. Think of it as a friendly sparring match."
Pairing
Pair students from different countries wherever possible. The cultural gap is the point — each side will ask questions the other would never ask itself. If numbers are uneven, the Assistant joins as Challenger (never Presenter — students must do the speaking).

6. Practice 1 — Challenger Questions & Example

Challenger Question Sequence — ask in this order

  1. "Why do you think so? Is it always true?"
    → fills Reason in the Logic Map
  2. "Can you give me a real example — from your own life?"
    → fills Example
  3. "What would someone who disagrees say? How would you respond?"
    → fills Counter-argument and Response
  4. "Is your example specific enough? Can you add one detail?"
    → sharpens the Example
  If all four are done and time remains, you may ask freely.

Logic Map Template

THESIS: ____________________________ ├─ Reason 1: ______________________ │ └─ Example: ___________________ │ └─ Counter-argument: _______________ └─ My response: ________________

Example Challenger Exchange

A: "My opinion is that university should be free for everyone."

B (Q1): "Why? Is it always a good idea?"

A: "Because then poor students can also go to university."

B (Q2): "Can you give me a real example from your life?"

A: "A friend of mine was very smart, but her family could not pay. She chose a cheaper school."

B (Q3): "What would someone who disagrees say?"

A: "Hmm… maybe that taxes would go up. But I think the long-term benefit is bigger."

B (Q4): "Is your example specific enough? Can you add a detail?"

A: "She wanted to study medicine, but ended up studying business at a local college because of fees."

B: "Good — your Map is strong now. Write it down."

Demo before breakout (~1 min)
Read the dialogue aloud with the Assistant playing A. Emphasize the question numbers: "Notice B is following the sequence — Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4. Each question fills a different part of the Map. By the time Q4 is done, the Map is complete."
Why a fixed sequence?
A fixed sequence solves two problems: (1) it prevents "polite agreement" — each question requires the Presenter to justify, exemplify, and face counter-arguments, and (2) it keeps the activity on time — four questions in six minutes is natural pacing. If pairs finish early, they may continue freely.
During breakout — what to listen for
✓ Is the Challenger actually challenging, or just being polite?
✓ Is the Presenter's argument changing under questioning? (That is the success signal.)
✓ Note 1–2 Logic Maps that visibly evolved — you will show these afterwards.
⚠ If a pair has collapsed into "yes, I agree with you too," jump in: "B, I haven't heard you disagree yet. Ask A — what if rich families also got it for free? Is that fair?"
After Practice 1 — main room (~2 mins)
"Pick 1–2 Logic Maps that visibly evolved. Show them: 'A started with free for everyone. After 4 minutes with B, it became free for students who pass an academic threshold, because talent should not be wasted. That is what challenge does. Now it is ready to be written.'"

7. Practice 2 — Micro-Drafting (8 mins)

Use your Logic Map refined with your partner in Practice 1 to write your Introduction + Body 1 in the shared Google Doc. Target: about 100 words.

Paragraph Frame

Introduction (2–3 sentences):

"The question of whether ______ is widely debated. O In my view, ______, because ______."

Body 1 (4–5 sentences):

R "To begin with, ______. E For instance, ______. S Consequently, ______, and this is why I would argue that ______."

  Write in the shared doc — in the next activity, a new partner will read your draft.
Focus on Introduction + Body 1 only. Do not include the Counter-argument — that belongs in Body 2.
~30 sec Setup
"Now you write. Use the Logic Map you refined with your partner. Only Introduction and Body 1 — about 100 words. You have 8 minutes. Use the Paragraph Frame on screen if you need a scaffold. Write in the shared Google Doc. You do not need a perfect essay — convert your spoken argument into written form. Remember: do not include the Counter-argument — that is for Body 2."
Stay in main room during drafting
Students write silently in the shared doc (muted). Keep them in the main room — this lets you announce "2 minutes left" clearly and makes the partner switch for Peer Review fast and clean.
During drafting — what to do
✓ Monitor students' Google Docs to check progress. Do NOT correct.
✓ If stuck: "What did your partner push you on earlier? Start from that sentence."
✓ If finished very early: "Good. Now read it silently to yourself. Does every sentence do a job?"
⚠ 8 minutes is the target, and time pressure is part of the training. However, if all students finish early, confirm and move to Peer Review — no need to wait out the clock.

7. Practice 2 — Peer Review with a New Partner (6 mins)

You will now be paired with a different partner from Practice 1. After moving to your breakout room, read your new partner's draft in the shared doc, and prepare oral feedback using the checklist below. See the suggested process on the right.

  • Is the Opinion clear in the Introduction?
  • Does Body 1 contain all four O-R-E-S elements?
  • Is the Example specific, or just general? ("my friend" alone is not specific.)
  • Are there linking words? (Firstly, For instance, Consequently…)
  • Does the Summary actually close the argument?

Feedback Phrases

Positive — "Your [Opinion / Reason / Example] was very clear because…"
Suggestion — "One thing that could be stronger is… — maybe you could add…"

Suggested Process

  1. Silent reading (2 min)
    Read your partner's draft. Use the checklist to prepare one positive comment and one suggestion.
  2. A gives oral feedback (2 min)
    A shares feedback with B. You may also type comments into B's Google Doc.
  3. B gives oral feedback (2 min)
    B shares feedback with A. Same format.
When finished: Return to main room — instructor highlights (2 mins)
~1 min Setup — announce the partner switch
"Drafting time is over, even if you are not finished. Now you are switching to a new partner — a fresh pair of eyes. Move to your assigned breakout room. First, spend 2 minutes reading your partner's draft silently and preparing your feedback. Then take turns giving oral feedback — 2 minutes each. You can also type comments directly into their Google Doc."
During peer review — what to listen for
✓ Are students giving specific feedback based on the checklist, or vague praise? Specific is the goal.
✓ The hardest thing is saying "your Example was too general." Model it yourself if a room is stuck.
After — main room highlights (~2 mins)
"Share one strong sentence you saw — quote directly. Then share one common improvement (anonymous). Example: 'A few of you wrote a strong Reason, but the Example was just my friend. Next time, add one detail — what subject, what happened. Details make examples believable.'"
⚠ Do not correct grammar or vocabulary now. Today is logic and cohesion only.
