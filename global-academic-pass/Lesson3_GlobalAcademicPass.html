4. Key Concept: Three Strategies for Unexpected Questions (8 mins)

When a question catches you off guard, use one of these three to stay structured. They are not tricks — they are tools to keep your O-R-E-S alive under pressure.

  • Buy Time — Give yourself a moment to think without going silent. "That's an interesting question — let me think for a moment."
  • Bridge — Connect the question to something you can talk about. "I'm not sure about that exactly, but in my own experience…"
  • Hedge — Soften your claim when you are unsure, instead of pretending to be certain. "It could be argued that…"

Demo

Instructor & Assistant: "Weak Answer" vs. "Rebuilt with Strategies"

Q: "But isn't using AI just cheating?"

A: (long pause) "Uhhh... I don't know. Maybe."

→ How can we rebuild this with the three strategies?

Note: The full phrase list for each strategy is on this slide deck — keep it open while you work.
~4 mins Explaining the three strategies
"Three strategies, three different problems. Buy Time is for when your brain just needs two seconds to catch up — instead of going silent, you say something like 'That's an interesting question, let me think for a moment.' Bridge is for when you genuinely cannot answer the exact question, but you can say something related. Hedge is for when you have an opinion but you're not 100% sure — you soften it instead of pretending to be certain."
Why these three, in this order
The order is roughly how much you commit. Buy Time is just a pause-with-words. Bridge changes the question slightly. Hedge actually answers but with caution. Students will naturally combine them — that is fine and encouraged.
Demo — Weak Answer first
"Watch this. [Assistant], ask me: 'But isn't using AI just cheating?'" — Assistant asks. You reply with the weak version on screen, with a long pause and the contradictions. Then look at students. "What's wrong with that answer?"
Let students respond. Accept answers like "no structure," "silence," "no opinion." Then ask: "Which of today's three strategies could have helped?" Most will say Buy Time. Then move to Slide 5b for the rebuilt version.

Same question — "But isn't using AI just cheating?" — rebuilt with Buy Time → Bridge → O-R-E-S. The structure didn't disappear; the strategies bought the time and the bridge to get into O-R-E-S.

Model Response

Buy Time"That's a fair question — let me think about that for a moment."
Bridge"I'm not sure I'd call it cheating in every case. Let me explain how I see it."
OIn my view, whether AI use is cheating depends on what the student does with it.
RThe main reason is that AI can be used to learn, or to avoid learning — those are very different actions.
EFor instance, when I use AI to explain a math concept and then solve the problem myself, I am studying. If I copy the answer without reading it, I am not.
SThat is why I would argue the issue is not the tool — it is how students choose to use it.
Delivering the Model Response
"Now let me try again — same question, but using the strategies." — Read the response naturally as connected speech, not block by block. Buy time first, then bridge, then go straight into O-R-E-S as usual.

Key message
"You don't need to use all three strategies every time. Pick the one that fits. Sometimes you only need Buy Time. Sometimes all three. The point is — you never go silent, and your O-R-E-S never collapses."

5. Academic Vocabulary

Continuing from Lesson 1 — three more high-frequency words to upgrade. Small changes, sharper academic tone:

Instead of "important"

  • Crucial — "It is crucial that..." "It is crucial that students learn to think for themselves."
  • Significant — "...has a significant impact on..." "AI has a significant impact on how students study."
  • Essential — "...is essential for..." "Critical thinking is essential for university success."

Instead of "many people / many students"

  • The majority of — "The majority of students..." "The majority of students in my class use AI for homework."
  • A considerable number of "A considerable number of teachers now allow it in class."
  • Most — "Most teenagers I know..." "Most teenagers I know rely on AI for at least one subject."

Instead of "use":  "Employ"  /  "Utilize"  /  "Rely on"  "Students employ AI to summarise long articles."

~4 mins Introduction
"Continuing the Academic Vocabulary work from Lesson 1. Today, three more high-frequency words: important, many people, and use. Every one of you reaches for these without thinking — today we upgrade them."
Walking through the vocabulary
"Instead of 'important,' try 'crucial' — it is crucial that students learn to think for themselves. Or 'significant' — AI has a significant impact on how students study. Instead of 'many people,' 'the majority of' — the majority of students in my class use AI. And instead of 'use,' try 'employ,' 'utilize,' or 'rely on' — students rely on AI when they get stuck."
Read two or three examples aloud — you do not need to cover all of them. Students will pick up the rest through use in Session 2.
Transition into Session 1
"In Session 2, your challenge is to use at least two of these words in your O-R-E-S response. Do not force them — let them come naturally. For Session 1, just standard O-R-E-S — let's start there."

6. 1-on-1 Exchange — Session 1 (15 mins)

PROMPT

"Should high schools allow students to use AI tools (like ChatGPT) for homework? What is your opinion?"

Steps

  • A: Share opinion using O-R-E-S
  • B: Ask one follow-up question
  • A: Respond → "How about you?"
  • B: Share opinion using O-R-E-S
  • A: Ask one follow-up question
  • Open discussion — agree, disagree, cultural differences
  • Tell your partner one thing they did well

Follow-up question ideas

  • "How is this different in your country?"
  • "Can you give a specific example?"
  • "What would be one disadvantage of that?"
  • "Do most students in your school feel the same?"
  B may also ask tougher, unexpected questions. If A gets one, A uses today's strategies — Buy Time → Bridge → Hedge (Slide 5).
When finished: Return to main room — instructor picks 1-2 expressions to highlight (1-2 mins)
15 mins Setup
"Okay, into breakout rooms. Today's first prompt is on the screen — AI tools for homework. Person A starts by sharing their opinion using O-R-E-S. Person B listens, then asks one follow-up question. After A responds, A turns it around with 'How about you?' — and B does their O-R-E-S. After both, use the remaining time to discuss freely. Before you come back, tell your partner one specific thing they did well."
"One new thing today: B, you can ask a tougher question if you want — something unexpected. If you do, A — that's your chance to use today's strategies: Buy Time, Bridge, Hedge."
Pairing
Pair students from different countries where possible — the cultural contrast on AI use is rich. If numbers are uneven, the Assistant or Instructor steps in to keep all pairs 1-on-1.
During breakout — what to listen for
✓ Is O-R-E-S being used, or are students just chatting?
✓ Did anyone use a Buy Time / Bridge / Hedge phrase naturally? Note it.
✓ Note 1–2 strong expressions to share after.
⚠ If a pair is silent or stuck, prompt: "Try starting with 'In my view...' and finish that sentence first."
After Session 1 — feedback (1–2 mins)
"Welcome back. I want to highlight this expression I heard: [quote what you heard]. Notice how that follows the O-R-E-S structure clearly — well done."
One or two observations maximum. Save detailed feedback for after Session 2.

Logic Map for Next Week

Next week's class is Logic & Writing — we will turn a spoken argument into a written paragraph. To prepare, fill in the Logic Map Worksheet (we will share it separately) for the prompt below.

PROMPT

"Will young people who grow up with AI become smarter, or more dependent? Give two reasons for your opinion."

WHAT TO DO
Complete the four parts of the map: Thesis, one Reason, one Example, and one Counter-argument with your response.
WHAT NOT TO DO
You do not need to write the full paragraph at home. Short, specific lines on the map are more useful than long vague ones.

  Have your completed map ready before next week's class — we will build the written version together from it.

~1 min Delivery
"One last thing before we finish — your homework for next week. Next class is Logic & Writing, where we will take the thinking we practised today and turn it into a written academic paragraph. To prepare, use the Logic Map Worksheet PDF — we will share it with you separately. On page 2, fill in a Logic Map for the prompt on screen: whether young people who grow up with AI will become smarter, or more dependent. You do not need to write the full paragraph at home. Just complete the four parts: Thesis, Reason, Example, and one Counter-argument with your response. Have it ready for next week's class and we will build the paragraph together."
Why this prompt
This prompt is deliberately different from today's two Session prompts. It asks about a future-oriented question students cannot Google an easy answer to, and forces them to think about themselves and their generation. Strong material for next week's writing class.
Why this homework, not a full paragraph
The Logic Map is deliberately shorter than writing 100 words. It lowers the psychological barrier to doing homework, and it gives us a structured artefact to work with in next week's class — whether the student came prepared or not.
If students look uncertain
Point them to the worked example on page 1 of the Logic Map Worksheet. Remind them: short, specific lines are better than long vague ones. Four blanks, not four paragraphs.
Closing
"That is it for today. Thank you — see you next week for Logic & Writing."
