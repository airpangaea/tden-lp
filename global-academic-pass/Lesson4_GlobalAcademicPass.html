4. Key Concept: Concession Structure (10 mins)

A strong written argument does not pretend the other side does not exist. It names the opposing view first, then defends its own — calmly and clearly. This move is called concession.

  1. Acknowledge the other side first. Use a signal word: While, Although, It is true that…
  2. Pivot back to your view. Use a contrast signal: However, Nevertheless, …
  3. Give your stronger reason. The Reason after the pivot should be more substantial than the one you just acknowledged.
  4. Tone stays calm, not combative. You are not attacking the other side — you are showing you have considered it.
Today's target: Introduction (with a concession move) + Body 1 = about 100 words
~3 mins Delivery
"In Lesson 2, you learned to convert your spoken opinion into a written paragraph. Today we add one move that makes any written argument noticeably stronger — concession. Concession means: name the other side first, then explain why your side still wins. Without it, your writing sounds like you have not considered the opposing view. With it, you sound like someone who has."
Read the four points with one quick example each. The "aha" will come from the Weak/Strong demo on the next two slides — do not over-explain here.
Reassurance
"Target is still 100 words — same as Lesson 2. We are not writing more. We are writing one move better."
⚠ Do not introduce Body 2 or Conclusion. Today is still about the opening move, now with a concession built in.

4. Demo — Add Concession, Get a Strong Paragraph

Step 1: This writer's Logic Map already had a Counter-argument — they just did not use it in the paragraph.

THESIS: Young people growing up with AI will become smarter overall ├─ Reason 1: AI accelerates how fast we can learn new concepts │ └─ Example: Used ChatGPT to grasp a difficult math topic in 10 minutes └─ Counter: They may lose the ability to think through problems on their own └─ Response: This is a real risk, but only if AI replaces thinking — not if it supports it

Step 2: The rewrite uses the Counter-argument as a concession at the start:

Strong — with Concession

It is true that AI may weaken some young people's ability to think through problems on their own. However, O I would argue that, on balance, my generation will become smarter because of AI, not less capable. R The main reason is that AI accelerates how quickly we can grasp difficult concepts, freeing time for deeper thinking. E For instance, last month I used ChatGPT to understand a calculus topic in ten minutes that would have taken me an hour with a textbook — and I then spent that saved hour solving the practice problems myself. S Consequently, AI is a multiplier of effort, not a replacement for it, which is why I see my generation gaining intelligence rather than losing it.

  The Counter-argument from the Map became a concession at the start of the paragraph — not at the end. By acknowledging the other side first, the writer's own Opinion lands with more authority.
Note: The concession lives in the Introduction. The Body 1 still follows O-R-E-S as before — concession does not replace the structure, it strengthens its opening.
~4 mins The "aha" moment
Show the Logic Map first. Point at the Counter row: "Last time, in Lesson 2, we saved the Counter for Body 2 and did not write it. This time we are going to use it — but at the start of the paragraph, as a concession."
Then reveal the Strong paragraph. Read the first two sentences slowly: "'It is true that AI may weaken some young people's ability to think through problems on their own. However, I would argue that…' — that is the concession move. The writer names the other side first, then pivots."
Key message
"Look at the Logic Map again — it had a Counter-argument all along. The Weak paragraph ignored it. The Strong paragraph used it. Same evidence, very different effect on the reader. That is the lesson for today: do not hide from the other side. Name it, then defend your view."
Assistant Real Talk (~2 mins)
"Assistant — when you write a paper or an email where you know the reader might disagree, do you mention their view first, or just push your own? What works better in practice?"
Most people will say acknowledging the other side first works better — it sounds fair, not defensive. Reinforce: this is a general writing skill, not just IELTS. Job applications, university essays, business emails — all benefit from concession.
⚠ Do not break down every sentence of the model. Read it once, point at "It is true that…" and "However, I would argue that…", and move on.

5. Concession Toolkit

These phrases let you acknowledge the other side without giving up your own. Pair an opening signal with a pivot — that is the basic move.

Opening signals — to name the opposing view

"While it is true that…"  /  "Although some argue that…"  /  "Critics may say that…"

Pivot signals — to return to your view

"However, …"  /  "Nevertheless, …"  /  "That said, …"  /  "On balance, …"

Full concession patterns (memorise one)

"While X is a valid concern, Y is the more important factor."

"It is true that X. However, I would argue that Y."

"Although X may happen, Y outweighs this risk."

Cohesive devices — carried over from Lesson 2

"Firstly, …"  /  "For instance, …"  /  "Consequently, …"  /  "I would argue that…"

  This toolkit has been shared with you in advance — keep the slide deck open in another tab while you write.
~2 mins Delivery
"Concession works in two halves. First half: name the other side with an opening signal — 'While it is true that…', 'Although some argue that…'. Second half: pivot back to your view with 'However…' or 'On balance…'. The opening and the pivot are a pair — you need both, and they go together."
Read the three full patterns aloud once. Tell students: "Memorise one pattern that feels natural to you, and reuse it. You do not need three; one is enough for today."
Students already have the full slide deck — no need to share anything in chat.

6. Practice 1 — Challenger Questions & Example

Challenger Question Sequence — ask in this order

  1. "Why do you think so? Is it always true?"
    → fills Reason 1
  2. "Can you give a real example — from your life or someone you know?"
    → fills Example
  3. "What is the strongest reason someone might disagree? How would you respond?"
    ★ Most important today → fills Counter + Response = your concession material
  4. "Could you sharpen the example, or add one more detail?"
    → sharpens the Example

Logic Map Template

THESIS: ____________________________ ├─ Reason 1: ______________________ │ └─ Example: ___________________ └─ Counter-argument: _______________ └─ My response: ________________

Example — Focus on Q3

B (Q1): Why?  →  A: AI helps us learn faster.

B (Q2): Real example?  →  A: Used ChatGPT to grasp math in 10 min.

★ Q3 — Concession is born here:

B: "What is the strongest reason someone might disagree?"

A: "Maybe… that we stop thinking for ourselves and just copy answers."

B: "Good. How do you respond?"

A: "That risk is real, but only if AI replaces thinking, not if it supports it."

B (Q4): Sharper example?  →  A: After understanding, I solved problems myself — AI didn't.

  The Q3 exchange is what your concession sentence will be built from.
Quick walkthrough before breakout (~1 min)
"Look at the right side. Q1, Q2, Q4 are quick warm-ups — push for a reason, push for an example, push for one more detail. The important one is Q3, in the yellow box. That is where the Presenter is forced to think about the other side, and that is what becomes your concession sentence in the paragraph."
Do NOT read the whole dialogue aloud — students will not follow. Just point at the yellow Q3 box and read those four lines only.
Why a fixed sequence?
A fixed sequence solves two problems: (1) it prevents "polite agreement" — each question requires the Presenter to justify, exemplify, and face counter-arguments, and (2) it keeps the activity on time — four questions in six minutes is natural pacing. If pairs finish early, they may continue freely.
During breakout — what to listen for
✓ Is the Challenger actually challenging, or just being polite?
✓ At Q3, is the Presenter genuinely engaging with the other side — not just dismissing it?
✓ Note 1–2 Logic Maps where the Counter-argument got noticeably stronger — you will show these afterwards.
⚠ If a pair has collapsed into "yes, I agree with you too," jump in with a sharper Q3: "B, ask A — what if AI makes us so lazy we cannot solve problems without it? How does A respond to that?"
After Practice 1 — main room (~2 mins)
"Pick 1–2 Logic Maps where the Counter-row visibly evolved. Show them: 'A started with "AI helps us learn." After 4 minutes with B, A added a real concession — that AI use is helpful only when it supports thinking, not when it replaces it. That nuance is what makes a Strong paragraph. Now it is ready to be written.'"

7. Practice 2 — Peer Review with a New Partner (6 mins)

You will now be paired with a different partner from Practice 1. After moving to your breakout room, read your new partner's draft in the shared doc, and prepare oral feedback using the checklist below. See the suggested process on the right.

  • Does the Introduction name the opposing view first (e.g., "It is true that…", "While…")?
  • Is there a clear pivot back to the writer's view (e.g., "However…", "Nevertheless…")?
  • Is the Opinion clear after the pivot?
  • Does Body 1 still contain all four O-R-E-S elements?
  • Is the Example specific, not general?

Feedback Phrases

Positive — "Your [Opinion / Reason / Example] was very clear because…"
Suggestion — "One thing that could be stronger is… — maybe you could add…"

Suggested Process

  1. Silent reading (2 min)
    Read your partner's draft. Use the checklist to prepare one positive comment and one suggestion.
  2. A gives oral feedback (2 min)
    A shares feedback with B. You may also type comments into B's Google Doc.
  3. B gives oral feedback (2 min)
    B shares feedback with A. Same format.
When finished: Return to main room — instructor highlights (2 mins)
~1 min Setup — announce the partner switch
"Drafting time is over, even if you are not finished. Now you are switching to a new partner — a fresh pair of eyes. Move to your assigned breakout room. First, spend 2 minutes reading your partner's draft silently and preparing your feedback. Then take turns giving oral feedback — 2 minutes each. You can also type comments directly into their Google Doc."
During peer review — what to listen for
✓ Are students specifically commenting on the concession move — or just on general O-R-E-S? Today's focus is the concession.
✓ If a draft has no concession at all, the reviewer should flag that as their main suggestion.
After — main room highlights (~2 mins)
"Share one strong concession opening you saw — quote directly. Then share one common improvement (anonymous). Example: 'A few of you wrote a Counter-argument but then dismissed it too quickly — "It is true that AI makes us lazy, but it is fine." That is not a real concession; it is a brush-off. A real concession takes the other side seriously for one sentence before pivoting.'"
⚠ Do not correct grammar or vocabulary now. Today is logic and concession only.
